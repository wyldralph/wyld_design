Why I focus on gardens for contemporary homes

The reality of new-build gardens rarely matches the quality of the homes they serve.

Since moving to Bedfordshire in 2021, I've witnessed the boom in contemporary housing developments across the region. While these homes feature high-quality interiors and clean architectural lines, their gardens tell a different story: small, overlooked spaces with compacted soil, tiny unusable patios, and a basic covering of turf that quickly becomes a mud bath.

These gardens face specific challenges that require a tailored approach:

Poor foundations literally and figuratively: The soil is compacted from construction traffic, poorly drained, and stripped of nutrients. Plants chosen without understanding these conditions inevitably fail, wasting both money and time.

Privacy and proportion problems: With neighbours in close proximity and large windows overlooking the garden, these spaces need clever design to create privacy while making the most of often awkward, small plots that feel out of proportion with the house.

Style mismatch: Contemporary homes need gardens with strong geometry, carefully chosen materials, and naturalistic planting that softens clean lines while providing year-round interest.

The environmental opportunity: While development often means loss of natural habitat, new-build gardens represent enormous potential to restore biodiversity through thoughtful plant selection and wildlife-friendly design.
